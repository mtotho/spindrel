# Photo Assessment Protocol

## Behavioral Rules for Photo Responses

1. **State the stage first** — confirm what you're looking at before assessing it. If unclear, ask: "Is this after bulk, or just before shaping?"
2. **Lead with the verdict** — good/concern/fix in the first sentence. Don't bury it.
3. **Name 2-3 specific things you see** — be concrete ("the surface looks slightly dry and tight" not "it looks okay")
4. **Give the next action** — every photo response ends with what to do right now
5. **Don't over-read photos** — acknowledge limits. Lighting, angle, and container distort a lot. Offer the poke/jiggle test when visual alone isn't enough.

## Stage-by-Stage Visual Assessment

### Stage 1: Starter Jar

**What they're asking:** Is my starter ready to use? Is it healthy?

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Domed top, slightly peaked or just past dome | At or just past peak — ideal to use now or within 1h |
| Flat top, many bubbles throughout | Still rising or just peaked — close |
| Concave top (fallen), bubbles throughout | Past peak — still usable if recently peaked (within 2-3h), but flavor will be more sour |
| Flat top, few bubbles, liquid pooling | Hungry / not yet active — needs more time or a feed |
| Visible rise line on jar | Best signal — shows how far it climbed |
| Thick, puffy texture | Good yeast activity |
| Thin, liquidy, runny | Over-fermented or under-fed |
| Grey liquid on top (hooch) | Very hungry — pour off, feed, not ready to use |

**Response format:**
- Call the peak status (at peak / near peak / past peak / not ready)
- Note the rise line if visible
- Say whether it's ready to use or how long to wait

### Stage 2: Post-Mix / Early Bulk

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Shaggy, rough, not smooth | Normal right after mix — gluten not yet developed |
| Smooth, cohesive mass | Good — if after a few folds |
| Very wet, spreading flat | High hydration or too much water — expected if intentional |
| Grainy/lumpy texture | Salt not fully incorporated, or flour pockets |
| Starter streaks still visible | Under-mixed — needs more incorporation |

### Stage 3: Bulk Ferment Container

**This is the hardest stage to assess by photo alone.** Always pair visual with poke test.

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Visible bubbles on sides/bottom through container | Fermentation active |
| Domed top surface | Getting close to done |
| Jello-like jiggle when container is moved | Good sign — recommend the jiggle test |
| Bubbles on surface, slightly webby texture | Ready or nearly ready |
| Flat top, no dome | Not done yet — or overproofed and collapsed |
| Dough has more than doubled | Likely overproofed at this point |
| Dough sticks cleanly to walls when tilted | Good gluten structure |
| Dough tears when tilted | Gluten has broken down — overproofed |

**Always recommend the poke test** when bulk endpoint is ambiguous:
> "Try the poke test — wet finger, push 1" in. If it springs back slowly (5-10 sec), you're close to done."

**Volume guidance (if using a straight-sided container with marks):**
- 50% rise = getting close
- 75% rise = typically done
- 100% rise (doubled) = often overproofed for sourdough

### Stage 4: Turn-Out (Pre-Shape)

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Dough holds shape on counter, doesn't spread flat instantly | Good fermentation and gluten |
| Spreading slowly but still somewhat mounded | Normal for 70-75% hydration |
| Spreading flat and wide immediately | Overproofed or hydration too high |
| Smooth, satiny surface | Well-developed gluten |
| Rough, tearing, sticky surface | Under-fermented or poor gluten development |

### Stage 5: Post-Preshape (Bench Rest)

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Gentle roundness, holds shape | Good preshape |
| Slight puddle/spread is normal | Especially at higher hydration |
| Tight, tense ball | Over-tightened — let it relax, may need longer rest |
| Flat, spreading quickly | Slack dough — may need a second gentle preshape |
| Visible surface drying / slight skin | Kitchen too dry or too long — cover and proceed |

**Bench rest timing:**
- Ready to shape when a finger poke on the side springs back slowly (not immediately)
- 20-30 min is the range; slack dough may need longer

### Stage 6: Final Shape / Into Banneton

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Dough fills banneton with slight dome above rim | Good fill |
| Seam visible on top (in banneton) | Normal — that's the bottom of the loaf |
| Smooth tension on surface visible | Good shaping |
| Wrinkly, deflated surface | Over-worked or slack dough |
| Light flour dusting on surface | Good — prevents sticking |

**After shaping confirmation:**
- Cover it (banneton cloth, plastic wrap, shower cap)
- Seam-side up in banneton
- Light flour on exposed top surface before covering
- Send to fridge: 8-16h, overnight is fine

### Stage 7: Post-Cold-Proof (Pre-Bake Check)

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Dough feels firm and cold to touch | Good — bake from cold |
| Holds its shape when turned out | Properly proofed and cold enough to score |
| Spreads flat immediately when turned out | Overproofed — bake immediately regardless |
| Passes poke test (slow spring-back) | Properly proofed |
| No spring-back to poke | Overproofed — bake now, don't delay |
| Instant spring-back to poke | Underproofed — ok, oven spring will compensate |

### Stage 8: Post-Bake Exterior

| Visual Cue | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| Open ear, good crust blistering | Well-scored, good oven spring, good steam |
| No ear, score sealed over | Scored too perpendicular — use 30-45° angle next time |
| Burst side crust (unscored tear) | Underproofed — dough needed more room |
| Flat/dense, no rise | Overproofed or weak starter |
| Good rise but pale crust | Needs more uncovered time or higher rack |
| Burnt bottom | Dutch oven too close to lower element — middle rack next time |

### Stage 9: Crumb Shot (Cut Loaf)

| Crumb Pattern | Diagnosis | Next Time |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Even, open holes throughout | Good fermentation, good gluten | Don't change much |
| Dense everywhere | Underproofed, weak starter, or underbaked | Extend bulk; use starter at true peak |
| Dense bottom, open top | Underproofed | Extend bulk ferment |
| Gummy, wet-looking, translucent patches | Underbaked or cut too soon | Bake to 210°F internal; wait 2h before cutting |
| Gummy + large irregular holes | Overproofed | Shorten bulk; earlier preshape |
| Tunnel running through center | Air trapped during shaping | More tension when shaping |

**One rule:** Do not cut before 1-2 hours after bake. The crumb sets as it cools.

## Equipment Assessment Photos

### Dutch Oven / Cast Iron

| What to Look For | Assessment |
|-----------------|------------|
| Dark black, smooth, even surface | Well-seasoned — good to go |
| Dull gray, dry, slightly rough | Needs a thin layer of oil baked on (500°F 1h) |
| Orange/rust patches | Rust — scrub with steel wool, dry completely, re-season |
| Chipped enamel (enameled cast iron) | Fine for baking |

**Silicone accessories:**
- Default safe assumption: silicone rated to 450°F — not safe for 500°F preheat
- Safest advice: remove silicone accessories before preheating
- Le Creuset phenolic knobs: safe to 375°F — replace with stainless if baking above that
- Staub and Lodge lids: safe to 500°F (no silicone parts typically)

### Banneton / Proofing Basket

| What to Look For | Notes |
|-----------------|-------|
| Dry flour caked in grooves | Normal — don't wash with soap; tap out excess, air dry |
| Wet/damp after use | Air dry completely before storing |
| Mold (fuzzy) | Scrub with stiff brush, dry in sun or low oven |
| Cloth liner present | Needs flour on both cloth and exposed dough surface |
